# Economic Cycles — SportMind Macro Intelligence

Recessions, consumer spending contractions, and inflation affect sports revenues
through two primary channels: consumer discretionary spending (tickets, merchandise,
subscriptions) and corporate spending (sponsorship, hospitality, naming rights).
The impact varies significantly by sport affluence level and revenue structure.

Load alongside `macro/macro-overview.md` for the full macro framework.

## The discretionary spending hierarchy

Not all sports are equally affected by recessions. Consumer spending on sport follows
a hierarchy based on price point and psychological essentiality:

```
MOST RESILIENT (fans prioritise even under financial pressure):
  Football (top leagues): Season tickets often paid in advance; strong identity attachment;
                           fans will cut other spending before their football
  American football (NFL): Deep cultural identity; Super Bowl events maintained even in recession
  Local team sports generally: Community identity makes attendance feel essential

MODERATELY AFFECTED:
  Premium ticket events (Champions League, Grand Slam tennis): Second purchase decisions;
                                                               sponsors review budgets
  Golf (The Masters hospitality): Corporate hospitality budgets cut first in recession
  Formula 1 (Paddock Club, premium hospitality): High-price corporate product; recedes faster

MOST VULNERABLE:
  Premium individual sports (golf, tennis, equestrian): Higher price points; affluent
                                                          audience still makes rational cuts
  Horse racing hospitality: Corporate entertainment budget; first to be cut
  Combat sports PPV: Discretionary single-purchase; cancelled faster than subscription

FAN TOKEN IMPLICATION:
  Recessions contract CHZ purchasing budgets moderately
  Fans may hold existing tokens (sunk cost; community value) but reduce new purchases
  New token launches face harder market conditions (consumer caution)
  Premium/hospitality token benefits may attract fewer buyers in recession
```

## Sponsorship lag — the delayed recession signal

Corporate sponsorship follows a different timeline from consumer spending:

```
SPONSORSHIP RECESSION PATTERN:
  Year 1 of recession: Sponsorships continue (multi-year contracts; brands honour commitments)
  Year 2 of recession: Renewals negotiated down 10–25%; some non-renewal
  Year 3 of recession: New sponsorship deals significantly reduced; discretionary brand spend cut
  Recovery Year 1: Brands cautious; sponsorship budgets slow to recover
  Recovery Year 2+: Sponsorship normalises and begins to grow

IMPLICATION: A recession beginning in 2024 may not be fully visible in
sports sponsorship revenues until 2025-26. Token holders should model
sponsorship as a lagging indicator of economic conditions, not a coincident one.

SPORT-SPECIFIC SPONSORSHIP VULNERABILITY:
  Most vulnerable: Sports where sponsorship is >40% of revenue (cycling, tennis, athletics)
  Moderately vulnerable: Sports where sponsorship is 25-40% (rugby union, MMA, esports)
  Most resilient: Sports where broadcast revenue dominates (NFL, Premier League, NBA)
```

## Inflation effects on sport

```
INPUT COST INFLATION:
  Player wages: Football, basketball have upward wage spirals regardless of economic cycle;
                player market is not directly tied to consumer inflation
  Venue operations: Energy costs, catering, security — affected by general inflation
  Travel costs: Grand Tour cycling, F1 logistics, international cricket — fuel price sensitive
  
CONSUMER PRICE INFLATION:
  Ticket prices: Sports have broadly passed inflation through to fans (especially premium seats)
  Subscription prices: Sky Sports, DAZN — inflation pass-through increasing churn risk
  Merchandise: Price-sensitive; fans more likely to reduce merchandise in high inflation
  
TOKEN MARKET IN INFLATION:
  Crypto assets (including CHZ) have historically been volatile in high-inflation periods
  Sometimes treated as inflation hedge (2020-21 narrative) — more often correlated with
  risk asset selloff when central banks raise rates aggressively (2022 experience)
  High interest rates → risk asset selloff → crypto decline → CHZ decline → token floor falls
```

## Recession monitoring signals

```
ECONOMIC EARLY WARNING SIGNALS:
  Yield curve inversion (2-year / 10-year US Treasury): Historically precedes recession
  Consumer confidence indices falling below 80 (Conference Board): Consumer spending risk
  PMI (Purchasing Managers' Index) below 50: Manufacturing contraction indicator
  Unemployment rising >0.5% from cycle low: Sahm Rule recession indicator

SPORT-SPECIFIC ECONOMIC SIGNALS:
  Premier League club revenue results: Annual financial disclosures; track aggregate growth
  NFL sponsor renewal rates: Indicator of corporate confidence in sports investment
  Ticket resale price premium (StubHub, Viagogo): Consumer discretionary willingness to pay
  Sports streaming churn rate: (Where disclosed) consumer budget pressure indicator

AGENT RULE: In a confirmed recession (two consecutive GDP quarters of negative growth),
  apply economic cycle modifier across all consumer-facing sports positions:
  
  Premium sports (F1, golf, tennis): × 0.85 on commercial signals
  Mass-market sports (football, rugby): × 0.92 on commercial signals
  Digital products (esports, fan tokens): × 0.95 (most resilient category)
  Crypto market (CHZ): Assess independently using macro-crypto-market-cycles.md
```

## Agent Context Summary

- Recession affects sports revenue with a lag (consumer contracts first; sponsorship later)
- Fan tokens are moderately recession-resilient vs physical sports revenue, but are
  affected by the crypto market correlation which amplifies recession impact
- Premium sports (golf, F1, tennis) face larger hospitality/sponsorship contractions than
  mass-market sports where fan identity spending is more resilient
- Monitor yield curve inversion → rising unemployment → consumer confidence → sports
  revenue as the leading indicator chain for sport-specific recession impact
