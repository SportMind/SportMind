# Climate Change and Extreme Weather — SportMind Macro Intelligence

Climate change is the longest-duration structural threat to the outdoor sports calendar
and venue infrastructure in this library. Unlike acute macro events (pandemics, wars),
climate disruption builds progressively and its effects compound. It is already affecting
match schedules, athlete performance, and venue viability — and the trajectory points
toward increasing disruption.

Load alongside `macro/macro-overview.md` for the full macro framework.

## Current documented climate impacts on sport

```
EXTREME HEAT:
  Australian Open (tennis): Extreme Heat Policy triggered multiple times
    2014: Multiple players retired mid-match; 73°C (163°F) court surface temperature recorded
    2019, 2022, 2023: Heat policy activations; reduced fan attendance on extreme days
    
  Tour de France (cycling): Stages shortened or modified due to extreme heat (2023+)
  
  Athletics World Championships (Doha 2019): Marathon held at midnight to avoid extreme heat;
    spectator attendance near-zero; athlete welfare criticised
    
  Tokyo Olympics 2021: Extreme heat and humidity; marathon relocated to Sapporo;
    multiple athletes treated for heat illness
    
  Cricket (India, Pakistan, Middle East): Regular postponements of morning/afternoon sessions
    becoming more frequent as temperatures rise

FLOODING AND EXTREME RAIN:
  British Open golf (various years): Play suspended due to flooding
  
  Grand National (horse racing): Course drainage improvements required; future events at risk
    if flood frequency increases at Aintree
    
  FIFA World Cup venues: Qatar desert location partially chosen to avoid rain risk;
    but broader venue portfolio globally faces increasing flood risk
    
  Rowing (Olympics): Course conditions affected by extreme weather events
  
  Cricket (county/domestic): UK county season increasingly disrupted by extreme rainfall events
```

## Sports most exposed to climate disruption

```
HIGHEST EXPOSURE (outdoor + fixed venue + weather-sensitive):

1. Cricket — Perhaps the most weather-sensitive sport in the library
   Multiple day formats particularly vulnerable (Test match)
   Dew factor (see domain skill) already a tactical variable; will intensify
   Pitch preparation increasingly challenged by extreme heat and drought
   
2. Horse racing — Fixed circuit venues; drainage infrastructure tested by flooding;
   Going conditions (going report) already a critical signal; more variable outcomes expected
   Major festivals (Cheltenham, Grand National) in regions with increasing flood risk
   
3. Cycling — Grand Tour route planning must account for extreme heat in mountainous stages;
   DNF risk from heat illness increasing; cobbled spring classics at risk from flooding
   
4. Golf — Open air; hot weather affects grass conditions; drought stress on courses;
   The Open Championship (UK links courses) increasingly susceptible to extreme wind and rain
   
5. Athletics (outdoor) — Marathon and field events at acute heat risk;
   governing bodies already implementing heat policies (start time changes, event cancellation)

MODERATE EXPOSURE:
  Tennis (outdoor venues — Wimbledon, Roland Garros, Australian Open)
  Football (outdoor venues with fixed kick-off times)
  Rugby union/league (outdoor stadia; player welfare protocols developing)
  
LOWER EXPOSURE (indoor or controlled environment):
  Ice hockey (indoor rink; climate controlled)
  Esports (fully indoor; no climate exposure)
  Snooker, darts (indoor arena)
  Boxing, MMA (indoor venue)
```

## Event cancellation risk by climate scenario

```
NEAR-TERM RISK (2025-2030):
  Australian Open: Extreme heat policy activations increasing; matches moved indoors
  Tour de France mountain stages: Shortened or rerouted due to heat risk
  Grand National: Flood risk at Aintree requires ongoing drainage investment
  
MEDIUM-TERM RISK (2030-2040):
  Wimbledon: UK summer heat extremes increasing; more Centre Court roof closures needed
  Olympics host city selection increasingly driven by climate suitability
  Multiple golf Major courses facing long-term water stress (grass condition maintenance)
  
LONG-TERM STRUCTURAL RISK (2040+):
  Some current outdoor venues become climatically unsuitable for summer sport
  Calendar restructuring across multiple sports to avoid peak heat periods
  Substantial investment required in venue climate adaptation or indoor conversion
```

## Climate as an investment and token signal

```
CLIMATE RISK PREMIUM ON TOKENS:

Clubs/sports at higher climate risk face:
  Potential cancellation of key revenue-generating events
  Adaptation costs that divert funds from squad/competitive investment
  Long-term uncertainty about venue viability
  
POSITIVE SIGNALS (climate adaptation):
  Venue climate investment announced (roof installation, drainage upgrade)
  Sport governing body announces calendar restructuring for climate
  Event moved indoors or to climate-appropriate time → reduced cancellation risk
  
AGENT RULE: For outdoor sports events in heat or flood-prone periods/regions:
  Check weather forecast before any position as standard practice
  Apply event cancellation risk modifier during extreme weather windows:
    Probability of cancellation > 20%: Reduce position by 25%
    Probability of cancellation > 40%: Reduce position by 50% or exit
    Cancellation confirmed: Assess held positions for settlement and reentry timing
```

## The positive climate signal — indoor migration

Climate disruption is accelerating the migration of some events and sports indoors,
which can be positive for both fan experience and commercial stability:

```
INDOOR MIGRATION EXAMPLES:
  Australian Open: Roof on all show courts; match continuity assured
  Roland Garros: Roof added to Philippe Chatrier court (2020)
  Wimbledon: Centre Court roof (2009); No. 1 Court roof added
  
COMMERCIAL IMPLICATION:
  Indoor/covered venues: More predictable match schedules → better broadcaster packages
                         → higher rights value → stronger financial base for clubs/tokens
  
  The cost of climate adaptation (installing roofs, improving drainage) is significant
  but the commercial return (schedule certainty, year-round venue use) is increasingly justified
```

STRUCTURAL CLIMATE MONITORING:

  Sports governing body climate strategies: FIFA, IOC, UEFA climate commitments
  Insurance industry sports climate risk assessments: Leading indicator of venue risk pricing
  Venue infrastructure investment announcements: Clubs adapting = lower long-term risk

## Agent Context Summary

- Cricket, horse racing, cycling, golf, and outdoor athletics are the sports with
  highest current climate exposure — check weather before any outdoor event position
- Event cancellation is the highest-impact single weather event for token positions;
  a cancelled Grand National or World Championship marathon has immediate negative impact
- Indoor migration is a positive long-term signal — venues investing in climate resilience
  are reducing their long-term commercial risk
- The Australian Open's comprehensive roof infrastructure is the gold standard in
  climate adaptation — it is now the most weather-secure of the four Grand Slams
