# Market Intelligence — SportMind Layer 4 Master Framework

The commercial context layer for SportMind. Answers the questions that Layers 1–3
cannot: how large is this market, who is the audience, what is the institutional
appetite for digital fan products, and how credible is a fan token for this sport?

---

## Why Layer 4 exists

Layers 1–3 teach an agent how a sport works, who is playing, and what is happening
on-chain. What they do not teach is *commercial gravity* — why some sports produce
$20B fan token events and others produce nothing despite having millions of passionate
fans.

An agent evaluating a fan token position in kabaddi needs different commercial context
than one evaluating a position in football. The sport mechanics may both be covered.
The on-chain signal may both show bullish HAS. But the underlying commercial ecosystems
are orders of magnitude apart in revenue, institutional sophistication, and token
market depth — and that changes how every signal should be weighted.

Layer 4 makes this commercial context explicit, consistent, and agent-readable.

---

## What Layer 4 covers

Each sport market file in this layer covers eight components:

### 1. Global Revenue and Broadcast Deals
Annual revenue of the sport's major competition(s), primary broadcast deal values
and partners, and revenue breakdown (broadcast vs matchday vs commercial). Where
data is unavailable, order-of-magnitude estimates with source notes.

Revenue is the commercial foundation that determines: how much money flows through
the sport, how large the institutional stakeholders are, and therefore how seriously
token infrastructure is likely to be taken by governing bodies and clubs.

### 2. Fanbase Size, Geography, and Demographic Profile
Global fan count estimates, core geographic markets (top 5 by fan concentration),
age profile (median fan age and youth index), and gender split where available.

Demographics matter for token prediction because: younger fanbases adopt digital
products faster, mobile-first markets (India, Southeast Asia) convert to on-chain
engagement more readily, and geographically concentrated fanbases create single-market
token concentration risk.

### 3. Digital and Social Engagement Index
Social media following across major platforms (aggregated where possible), engagement
rate benchmarks for the sport's athletes and clubs, streaming vs broadcast consumption
split, and mobile consumption percentage.

Digital engagement is the most reliable leading indicator of fan token potential.
A sport with 50M fans who primarily engage on mobile beats a sport with 200M fans
who primarily watch on television for token adoption purposes.

### 4. Institutional Blockchain and Fan Token Interest
Documented actions by the sport's governing body, leagues, and clubs regarding
blockchain, NFTs, fan tokens, or digital collectibles. Ranges from active Chiliz
partnerships (highest) to exploratory discussions (medium) to public scepticism or
explicit rejection (low).

This is a forward signal — institutional interest precedes token launches. Monitoring
it creates advance notice of token market expansion.

### 5. Fan Token Readiness Tier
A synthesised 1–4 classification combining all other components. See tier definitions
below. This is the single most actionable output of a market file — it tells an agent
at a glance where a sport sits in the commercial development arc.

### 6. Media Rights Trajectory
Whether broadcast and digital rights deals are growing, stable, or declining, and
the structural reasons behind the trajectory. Includes recent deal comparisons
(previous cycle vs current) and emerging platform dynamics (streaming rights,
social media rights, short-form content deals).

Media rights are a proxy for sport health. A sport losing ground on rights deals
is structurally weakening even if current engagement metrics look healthy. A sport
gaining rights value is expanding its commercial reach.

### 7. Token Catalyst Events
For sports without active tokens (Tier 2–4): what specific events would most likely
trigger a token launch. Ranges from a governing body partnership with Chiliz to a
single high-profile club making a unilateral move to a regulatory development that
opens the door.

For sports with active tokens (Tier 1): what events drive the next expansion phase
— more clubs, more nations, higher token market depth.

### 8. Competitor Landscape
Other digital fan engagement products competing in this sport's commercial space.
NFT collections, club-specific apps, fantasy leagues with digital assets, rival token
platforms (Socios vs Fan Bits vs others), and traditional loyalty programmes that
could be token-adjacent.

Understanding the competitor landscape tells an agent: is Chiliz/Socios the dominant
platform here or are there rivals? Is the sport's digital fan economy already crowded
or is there a clear entry point?

## Fan Token Readiness Tier Definitions

The tier system is the core output of Layer 4. Every agent loading a market file
should be able to read the tier and immediately understand the commercial context.

### Tier 1 — Active Token Ecosystem
**Definition:** Fan tokens are actively trading on a major platform (Chiliz/Socios
or equivalent) with real liquidity, a documented holder base, and live on-chain data.

**Characteristics:**
- Multiple clubs or organisations with active tokens
- Regular token holder events and utility (voting, exclusive content, etc.)
- Measurable HAS, TVI, and AELS data available via Layer 3 skills
- Institutional commitment confirmed (league or governing body endorsement, or
  multiple clubs independently active)

**Agent implication:** Layer 3 fan-token skills are fully applicable. Market file
provides context for sizing and sentiment baseline.

**Current Tier 1 sports:** Football, Basketball (NBA-adjacent), MMA (UFC/PFL),
Esports (CS2, LoL orgs), Formula 1 (constructor tokens), Cricket (PSL tokens,
growing IPL adjacent)

---

### Tier 2 — High Credibility, Near-Term (12–36 months)
**Definition:** No active token ecosystem, but the sport has the commercial
footprint, fanbase profile, and institutional signals that make a token launch
credible within the planning horizon.

**Characteristics:**
- Large, digitally engaged fanbase (>20M active social followers across sport)
- Revenue base sufficient to attract institutional blockchain partners (>$500M annually)
- At least one documented signal of institutional interest (partnership, pilot, or
  public statement from a governing body, major club, or player association)
- No structural barriers (regulatory, cultural, or economic) preventing token launch

**Agent implication:** Monitor institutional signals closely. Position early in
social and sentiment signals when launch is announced. Layer 1 and Layer 2 skills
are fully applicable for prediction; Layer 3 activates on token launch.

**Current Tier 2 sports:** MLB (baseball), NHL (ice hockey), AFL, MotoGP,
Rugby League (NRL), Handball (EHF), Kabaddi (PKL)

---

### Tier 3 — Viable but Longer Horizon (3–5 years)
**Definition:** Sufficient audience to support a token but lacks one or more of:
institutional interest, digital-first fanbase, revenue depth, or commercial
infrastructure for token utility.

**Characteristics:**
- Large total fanbase but lower digital engagement index
- Revenue base present but concentrated in a small number of stakeholders who
  may not be motivated by token infrastructure
- No clear institutional signal toward blockchain products
- Cultural or demographic headwinds (older fanbase, offline-first consumption)

**Agent implication:** No token prediction activity warranted. Monitor for
institutional interest signals that could upgrade to Tier 2. Layer 1 and Layer 2
applicable for general prediction intelligence.

**Current Tier 3 sports:** Golf, Tennis, Horse Racing, Boxing (sport-level),
Cycling, Rugby Union (club level), Athletics/Track and Field, Darts, Snooker,
Swimming, Winter Sports

---

### Tier 4 — Niche or Structurally Distant
**Definition:** Passionate following but economics of a fan token do not currently
work. Either the audience is too small for token market depth, the commercial
infrastructure is absent, or the sport is structurally incompatible with digital
fan engagement products.

**Characteristics:**
- Fanbase is regionally concentrated and/or small by global standards
- Revenue base insufficient to attract platform partners
- No plausible institutional pathway to token infrastructure in any planning horizon
- Sport may have cultural or governance characteristics that make token utility
  impractical (amateur-dominant, Olympic-cycle-only audience, etc.)

**Agent implication:** Exclude from any token-related intelligence. Layer 1 applicable
for general prediction in sports where prediction markets exist.

**Current Tier 4 sports:** Sailing, Rowing, Gymnastics, Weightlifting, Judo,
Taekwondo, Fencing, Curling, Squash, Badminton (outside Asia), Triathlon,
Swimming (open water), Table Tennis (outside China/Japan)

## Tier Upgrade and Downgrade Signals

Tiers are not permanent. Agents should monitor for events that signal a tier change:

### Upgrade signals (Tier moves up)
```
Tier 4 → Tier 3:
  Governing body announces partnership with a digital fan engagement platform
  Major club announces NFT or token pilot programme
  Sport's broadcast deal significantly increases (>30% growth)
  
Tier 3 → Tier 2:
  Governing body signs a formal blockchain/token partnership agreement
  3+ major clubs from the sport announce token intent simultaneously
  Major media platform (Amazon, DAZN, Apple) announces streaming deal including
  digital fan product rights
  Sport's revenue base crosses $1B+ annually
  
Tier 2 → Tier 1:
  First token actively trading with >$1M daily volume
  Chiliz/Socios signs formal league-level partnership
  Multiple clubs independently launch tokens within 6 months

### Downgrade signals (Tier moves down)
  Governing body publicly rejects cryptocurrency/token infrastructure
  Major clubs that launched tokens delist or abandon them
  Regulatory action in core market prevents token trading
  Sport's broadcast rights decline >20% in successive deal cycles
  Significant reputational event (match-fixing, governance scandal)
```

## How to use Layer 4 with Layers 1–3

Layer 4 is context, not a signal. It does not produce modifiers or scores.
It frames how heavily to weight signals from the other layers.

```
LOADING ORDER FOR FULL INTELLIGENCE:

1. market/{sport}/market-{sport}.md     ← Tier and commercial context first
2. sports/{sport}/sport-domain-{sport}.md ← How the sport works
3. athlete/{sport}/athlete-intel-{sport}.md ← Player intelligence
4. fan-token/* (if Tier 1)              ← On-chain intelligence only if active token

MARKET TIER × AGENT BEHAVIOUR:

Tier 1: Full Layer 1–4 stack. All signals applicable.
         Layer 3 HAS/AELS/APS scores are live and actionable.

Tier 2: Layers 1–2 only for prediction. Monitor Layer 4 for institutional signals.
         Pre-position in social and sentiment monitoring.
         Activate Layer 3 immediately when token launches.

Tier 3: Layers 1–2 only. No token intelligence applicable.
         Market file provides context for general prediction sizing.

Tier 4: Layer 1 only (where prediction markets exist for the sport).
         Market file is archival — do not monitor for token signals.
```

## Commercial Data Sources

Market intelligence relies on the following source categories. All figures are
estimates based on published reports and should be updated annually:

**Revenue and broadcast data:**
- Deloitte Annual Review of Football Finance (football)
- PwC Sports Outlook (multiple sports)
- Sportcal / SportsPro Media (broadcast deals globally)
- Forbes Valuations (team and franchise values)
- Official league financial disclosures where published

**Fanbase and engagement data:**
- Nielsen Fan Insights (fan counts, demographics)
- Sportradar / Statista Sports (social following, engagement)
- GlobalWebIndex (digital consumption by sport)
- Platform-published data (Meta, X/Twitter, YouTube Sport reports)

**Blockchain and institutional interest:**
- Chiliz/Socios official partnership announcements
- Press releases from governing bodies and major clubs
- CoinGecko / CoinMarketCap (active token data)
- SportsPro / SportBusiness (digital strategy reporting)

**Rights trajectory:**
- Sport Business Intelligence (rights deal tracker)
- Broadcast & Cable (US rights data)
- SVG Europe (European broadcast rights)

[Global fan count, top 5 markets, age profile, gender split]

[Social following, engagement rates, streaming vs broadcast, mobile %]
